# Robot Hardware Options

This section explores various humanoid robot platforms and hardware options available for implementing the concepts learned in this textbook, comparing different approaches and their trade-offs. Choosing the right robot platform is crucial for successful humanoid robotics development, affecting everything from development complexity to final capabilities.

## Commercial Humanoid Platforms

### NAO by SoftBank Robotics

NAO is one of the most popular humanoid robots for research and education:

| Specification | Details |
|---------------|---------|
| Height | 58 cm |
| Weight | 5.2 kg |
| Degrees of Freedom | 25 |
| Sensors | 2 cameras, 2 microphones, 9 tactile sensors, 2 ultrasound sensors |
| Computing | Intel Atom Z3745, 1.33 GHz quad-core |
| OS | NAOqi (based on Linux) |
| Price | ~$8,000-12,000 |

**Pros:**
- Mature ecosystem with extensive documentation
- ROS support available
- Proven platform for research and education
- Good community support

**Cons:**
- Discontinued as of 2023
- Limited computational power for advanced AI
- Expensive for the capabilities provided

### Pepper by SoftBank Robotics

Pepper is a more advanced humanoid platform focused on human interaction:

| Specification | Details |
|---------------|---------|
| Height | 120 cm |
| Weight | 28 kg |
| Degrees of Freedom | 20 |
| Sensors | 3D camera, 2D camera, 4 microphones, touch sensors |
| Computing | Intel Core i3, 2.2 GHz |
| OS | NAOqi (based on Linux) |
| Price | ~$20,000-30,000 |

**Pros:**
- Advanced human interaction capabilities
- Good for social robotics research
- Multiple sensors for perception
- Commercial support available

**Cons:**
- Expensive for research applications
- Limited mobility (wheeled base)
- Discontinued as of 2022

## Open Source Humanoid Platforms

### Poppy Project

The Poppy Project offers open-source 3D-printed humanoid robots:

| Model | Poppy Humanoid | Poppy Torso | Poppy Ergo Jr |
|-------|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Height | 80-100 cm | 40 cm | 20 cm |
| Weight | 2-3 kg | 1 kg | 0.5 kg |
| DOF | 24-26 | 6-12 | 6 |
| Actuators | Dynamixel servos | Dynamixel servos | Dynamixel servos |
| Price | ~$1,500-3,000 | ~$800-1,200 | ~$300-500 |

**Pros:**
- Open-source design files
- 3D-printable parts
- Active research community
- Educational focus
- Affordable compared to commercial options

**Cons:**
- Requires assembly and tuning
- Limited payload capacity
- Less robust than commercial robots
- Requires 3D printing capabilities

| Specification | Details |

- Mechanical design challenges
- No official support
- Requires 3D printing and electronics skills

## Custom Build Options

### Using Dynamixel Actuators

Dynamixel servos from Robotis are popular for custom builds:

| Model | AX-12A | RX-28 | RX-64 | MX-28 | MX-64 | MX-106 |
|-------|--------|-------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Torque (stall) | 1.5 Nm | 2.5 Nm | 4.2 Nm | 2.5 Nm | 6.0 Nm | 8.4 Nm |
| Speed | 37 RPM | 48 RPM | 53 RPM | 55 RPM | 51 RPM | 45 RPM |
| Price | ~$40 | ~$100 | ~$150 | ~$120 | ~$200 | ~$300 |
| Feedback | Position | Position, Speed | Position, Speed | Position, Speed, Load | Position, Speed, Load | Position, Speed, Load |

**Pros:**
- Precise control with built-in feedback
- Daisychain communication
- Good documentation and support
- Various torque options available

**Cons:**
- Expensive for multiple actuators
- Limited to Robotis ecosystem
- Protocol limitations
- Higher latency than direct control

### Using QDD Actuators

QDD (Quasi-Direct Drive) actuators offer high-performance options:

| Type | QDD-100 | QDD-200 | QDD-500 |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| Continuous Torque | 10 Nm | 20 Nm | 50 Nm |
| Peak Torque | 30 Nm | 60 Nm | 150 Nm |
| Gear Ratio | 90:1 | 90:1 | 90:1 |
| Weight | 1.2 kg | 1.8 kg | 3.5 kg |
| Price | ~$800 | ~$1,200 | ~$2,000 |

**Pros:**
- High torque density
- Low backlash
- Good for dynamic movements
- Precise control

**Cons:**
- Very expensive
- Requires custom control systems
- Limited availability
- Complex integration

## DIY Approaches

### 3D Printed Robot Kits

Several companies offer pre-designed 3D printed robot kits:

- **Lynxmotion**: Hexapod and biped kits with Arduino controllers
- **Trossen Robotics**: PhantomX, ReactorX arm kits
- **Robotis**: OP series with pre-designed frames

### Custom Mechanical Design

For advanced users, designing custom mechanical systems:

- **CAD Software**: Fusion 360, SolidWorks, FreeCAD
- **Manufacturing**: 3D printing, CNC machining, laser cutting
- **Materials**: PLA/PETG for 3D printing, aluminum for structural elements
- **Joints**: Ball bearings, bushings, custom gears

## Budget Considerations

### Entry-Level Options ($500-2,000)
- Arduino-based biped robots
- Simple servo-based designs
- InMoov or Poppy builds
- Educational kits

### Mid-Range Options ($2,000-10,000)
- Used NAO robots
- Custom builds with quality actuators
- Research platforms like Darwin OP
- Pre-designed kits with good DOF

### High-End Options ($10,000+)
- New commercial robots
- Custom builds with premium actuators
- Professional research platforms
- Industrial-grade systems

## Integration Considerations

### ROS Compatibility

Ensure your chosen platform supports ROS 2:
- Check for existing ROS packages
- Verify communication protocols
- Consider custom driver development
- Evaluate sensor integration complexity

### Sensor Integration

Plan for necessary sensors:
- Cameras for vision
- IMU for balance
- Force/torque sensors
- Tactile sensors for manipulation

### Power Management

Consider power requirements:
- Battery selection and placement
- Power distribution systems
- Battery management circuits
- Operational time requirements

## Development Timeline

### Ready-to-Use Platforms
- **Time to operation**: 1-2 weeks
- **Customization time**: 2-6 months
- **Best for**: Focusing on software/AI rather than hardware

### Kit-Based Platforms
- **Assembly time**: 1-4 weeks
- **Calibration time**: 1-2 weeks
- **Best for**: Learning both hardware and software

### Custom Builds
- **Design time**: 3-6 months
- **Assembly time**: 1-3 months
- **Tuning time**: 3-6 months
- **Best for**: Specialized applications

## Recommendations by Use Case

### Educational Institutions
- **Budget**: Start with Poppy or used NAO
- **Focus**: Software development and AI concepts
- **Consider**: Multiple smaller robots vs. one large one

### Research Labs
- **Budget**: Custom builds with quality components
- **Focus**: Specific research questions
- **Consider**: Modularity and extensibility

### Hobbyists
- **Budget**: InMoov or simple custom builds
- **Focus**: Learning and experimentation
- **Consider**: Community support and documentation

### Commercial Development
- **Budget**: Established platforms or custom solutions
- **Focus**: Reliability and performance
- **Consider**: Support, warranty, and long-term availability

:::tip
Start with an existing platform to learn the fundamentals before attempting custom builds. The mechanical design of humanoid robots is complex and time-consuming.
:::

:::info
The choice of robot platform significantly impacts your development timeline and capabilities. Consider your specific goals, budget, and technical expertise when making this important decision.
:::
